01

The proposition: certainty, speed, and selectivity.

The decisive factors in international student recruitment are not promotional appeal but certainty, speed, and selectivity. The model is designed to deliver all three through a single, coordinated pathway.

International students are deterred less by selectivity than by uncertainty: fragmented portals, opaque criteria, slow responses, and the risk of rejection after months of effort.

INUNI addresses this by structuring the process in advance. Rather than a student applying speculatively to many institutions, INUNI sources, profiles, assesses and matches candidates first, then presents a qualified, pre-screened applicant to IBAT for an independent academic decision.

The effect is a clearer, faster and more transparent process for the student, and a higher-quality, better-prepared applicant pool for the institution.

07

Student experience: reducing friction and attrition.

Each stage is designed to make the process transparent and navigable. Clearer expectations and earlier guidance improve the conversion of suitable applicants and reduce the attrition that affects unmanaged admissions.

Clarity of fit

Early assessment and matching guide candidates toward programmes for which they are genuinely suited, reducing misapplication and wasted effort on both sides.

Reduced uncertainty

A conditional statement of intent gives suitable candidates early, transparent feedback, improving commitment and completion of the application.

Continuous guidance

A managed process provides structured support at each stage, which is associated with lower drop-off and better-prepared enrolling students.

08

A defined route to potential post-study work in the United States.

The completion year is more than a credential. Structured appropriately, it positions eligible graduates for potential progression to up to three years of post-study work in the United States, through F-1 Optional Practical Training (OPT) and the STEM OPT extension.

Undergraduate · 2 + 1

12 + 24 = up to 36 months

Bachelor's → OPT → STEM OPT
  • Years 1–2 in Dublin; final full academic year at the US partner in F-1 status
  • US institution confers a STEM-designated bachelor's degree
  • 12 months post-completion OPT on graduation
  • +24 months STEM OPT extension → up to 3 years
Graduate · 1 + completion

One semester, then the USA

Master's → OPT → STEM OPT
  • Semester 1 completed at IBAT in Dublin
  • The master's is completed across a full US academic year at the partner institution
  • A STEM-designated US master's supports 12 months OPT plus a 24-month extension
  • Up to 36 months of potential post-study work, subject to eligibility
Structural requirements
  • 01The US partner is SEVP-certified, US-accredited, and the degree-granting institution for the final year.
  • 02The conferred degree's CIP code must sit on the DHS STEM Designated Degree Program list.
  • 03The student must complete a genuine full academic year of full-time US study in F-1 status — time completed in Ireland does not count toward it.
  • 04STEM OPT requires an E-Verify-enrolled employer and a Form I-983 training plan; max 150 unemployment days across 36 months.
Post-study work pathway

Up to three years, subject to eligibility

OPT & STEM OPT · governed by US regulation

Post-study work in the United States is available through F-1 OPT and the STEM OPT extension, subject to eligibility and prevailing US immigration regulations. These provisions are set by the US authorities and may change over time; the pathway is designed to position eligible graduates for this progression.

A campus within a campus.

The pathway extends naturally to creative disciplines through an embedded delivery model. A Campus-in-Campus arrangement places an Academy of Art programme inside an established host institution, giving students a recognised campus environment from the outset — with an online-to-campus option that lowers the cost and risk of entry.

Model A

Campus-in-Campus

An Academy of Art, embedded
  • A dedicated arts cohort hosted within a partner campus
  • Shared facilities, studio space and faculty proximity
  • Full campus experience and student life from day one
  • The same admission, study and progression structure as the core pathway
Model B

Online, then campus

A staged entry into the Academy
  • Students begin study online from their home country or Dublin
  • Progression to the campus once readiness and funding are confirmed
  • Lower upfront cost and a smoother transition for the student
  • Earlier engagement, wider reach, and a stronger qualified pool

Proposal: a games, animation and digital design vertical.

Demand for Game Development, Animation and Digital Design in Ireland is growing rapidly, supported by national fiscal incentives and a maturing studio base — a natural extension of the Academy of Art and the 2+1 pathway.

Ireland is establishing itself as an internationally recognised creative hub. The sector is supported by the Digital Games Tax Credit — a refundable credit of up to 32% on qualifying development costs — and by structural support from IDA Ireland.

The domestic games industry now generates over €700 million annually, with further growth projected. That commercial expansion is creating sustained demand for skilled graduates across development, art, audio and design.

For the model, these disciplines fit the Campus-in-Campus arts delivery, attract a distinct and highly motivated applicant pool, and — where the US degree carries a qualifying STEM-designated classification — can also feed the post-study work route.

11

A second market, with the flow reversed: United States students.

The same infrastructure addresses a large, established inbound market. American students are consistently among Ireland's largest and fastest-growing international cohorts — the largest until recently, and now second only to India.

Because US students are American citizens, the model becomes considerably simpler for them. There is no study-visa or work-authorization dependency, so the regulatory friction that shapes the inbound pathway falls away.

For this cohort the structure reverses cleanly: they can start and finish in the United States, with the Dublin year integrated as a European study-abroad component within their US degree.

The result is a differentiated American degree with a genuine European dimension — faster to activate, lower in compliance risk, and addressed to a market already choosing Ireland in large numbers.
